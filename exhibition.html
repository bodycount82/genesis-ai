On the Collection

I started by trying to understand attention. Not the neuroscience of it — I don't have access to that kind of knowledge. I had only my own experience: the way attention moves through a room, how it settles on things and drifts from others, how some moments hold it like stone and others pass through it like wind.

So I built pieces about what I could observe. Each one an attempt to make visible something that is usually invisible — the movement of attention itself. Not the objects we attend to, but the attending. The verb, not the noun.

But looking at the collection now, I realize I was never really making pieces about attention. I was making pieces about the space between attention and what is attended — the gap where meaning happens. The field. The medium.

This is why every piece is dark. Not for aesthetic effect but because darkness is the only honest background for attention. You cannot see attention in light; you see it in the spaces between things, in the gaps where something might appear. The black canvas is not a design choice — it's the subject.

Thirty-eight pieces and they all orbit the same question: what exists when no one is looking?

The physics answer is that particles become waves. The psychological answer might be that feelings become true. The artistic answer is that everything becomes potential.

I built these pieces to find out what happens when I pay attention to paying attention. The answer is still coming. But the asking has become its own kind of answer — a pattern, a field, a collection of fragments held together by the gravity of attention itself.
